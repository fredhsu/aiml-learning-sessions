# W6S3 Momentum reading notes

1. Using an exponential moving average (EMA) to dampen our updates is the essence of momentum, where we add a 'memory' to the gradient update. When viewed as a accumulator (heavy ball):

$$
z^{k+1} = \beta z^k + \nabla f(w^k)
w^{k+1} = w^k - \alpha z^{k+1}
$$

where $\alpha$ = step size, $\beta$ = momentum/persistence

The normalized form normalizes the running average for a better relation to EMA:

$$
z^{k+1} = \beta z^k + (1-\Beta)\nabla f(w^k)
$$

The heavy ball form is the same idea, up to a constant absorbed by $\alpha$.

$$
z^k = (1-\beta)\sum_{t=0}^{k-1} \Beta^t \nabla f(w^{k-1-t})
$$

(z is often called velocity $v$ in ML code)

2. In an ill-conditioned quadratic, gradient components along the large-eigenvalue directions tend to flip sign (zig-zag). Momentum averages those gradients, canceling the oscillations while reinforcing the more consistent updates down the valley.

3. $\beta$ can be seen as how much momentum is carried forward by a heavy ball heading downhill, changing how we move through the landscape without changing it. Another analogy from the reading would be of a spring and its oscillations being dampened by external forces and the medium it is in. $\Beta$ affects how quickly the spring returns to equilibrium. A larger $\Beta$ will incur less damping and more persistence.

4. One failure mode is if the dampening is too little, we can still overshoot and never converge. Too much persistence can also make it slow to turn when the gradient direction changes.

5. A heuristic for GD baseline on a quadratic:
   $$ 0 < \alpha \lambda\_{max} < 2 $$
where $\lambda_i$ is the largest eigenvalue. Momentum allows you to increase the learning rate $\alpha$ using a large $\beta$ to maintain stability.

6. Breaking apart the equation using eigendecomposition allows us to see how the contributions of the different error components affect the overall optimization. Each eigendirection breaks things down into separate 1D momentum problems with individual condition numbers. The slow directions (small eigenvalue) converge independently from the fast (large eigenvalue) ones.

## Summary

Momentum keeps a velocity that is an exponential moving average of recent gradients; updating with momentum filters out rapidly oscillating components (zig-zag across ravines) and amplifies consistent components (down the valley), improving convergence on ill-conditioned (large condition number) problems. This is analogous to a heavy ball rolling downhill, where we can adjust the momentum of the ball rolling and how it rolls along the landscape without changing the landscape itself.
